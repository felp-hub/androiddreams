Will Androids Dream of Electric Sheep?

How accurate is the portrayal of AI within science fiction?

credit

Through the lens of science fiction storytellers across the globe have envisioned worlds of advanced technology and unfathomable futures. Worlds where society itself no longer resembles the world of reality.

How could Johannes Kepler ever envision man walking on the moon when he wrote 'Somnium' in 1634. Yet over 300 years later, Neil Armstrong and Buzz Aldrin became the first men to do just that.

For years Sci-Fi has been able to predict, predate and portray real world developments, with varying degrees of success. This writing will focus on the accuracy of science fiction in portraying real-world developments of artificial intelligence.

It's believed that artificial intelligence first appeared in fiction over two hundred years ago with Mary Shelly's 'Frankenstein' (1818). This precedes the first real world studies of artificially intelligent machines by over a hundred and fifty years.

To answer the question posed by this essay, a number of films, novels and games were selected to act as a timeline, charting the ideas and appearances of AI in Sci-Fi from Isaac Asimov's 'Runaround' written in 1942, to Denis Villeneuve's 2017 film, 'Blade Runner 2049.''

Plotting the course

When choosing what stories to examine for this study the following criteria was considered:

  • Artificial intelligences prominence within the fiction
  • The social significance of the fiction
  • The inspiration for the fiction

While the chosen stories may not meet all the listed criteria, they all met enough to be relevant and provide significant instances of AI to examine when asking how accurate Sci-Fi has been in portraying artificial intelligence.

Turing to terminators

Artificial Intelligence exploded onto screens during the 80s, when Rick Deckard set out to retire the Nexus-6 Replicants in Ridley Scott's cult classic 'Blade Runner' (1982) and the despotic Skynet began its crusade to destroy human life in James Cameron's 'The Terminator' (1984).

But before the Terminator's T-800's and Gibson's hyper-intelligent Neuromancer came Alan Turing. The brilliant British scientist, who after working as a codebreaker during the second world war, began to 'wonder about the intelligence of the machines used to break the Enigma code'. A few years later Turing published his seminal article 'Computing Machinery and Intelligence' (1950) hailed by many as one of the first articles describing artificial intelligence. Six years after, the phrase artificial intelligence was coined when Marvin Minsky and John McCarthy hosted an eight weeklong Dartmouth Summer Research Project on Artificial Intelligence (DSRPAI). Minsky would go on to cofound the AI lab at MIT in 1970 and continue to research and develop artificial intelligence throughout his career.

Alan Turing: Godrey Argent Studio, via The Royal Society

Runaround Robots

Alongside the rise of AI as an academic study in the real world, many of the landmark themes of AI fiction came into being.

In ‘Runaround’ (1942), Isaac Asimov introduced the world to his “Three Laws of Robotics”, telling a story about Speedy, an AI robot becoming caught in a loop and running around in a circle as a result of these three laws. At the time of writing, no such technology existed of course, but Marvin Minsky discussed how ‘Runaround’ had a lasting impact on his life and work, remarking that after reading it he “never stopped thinking about how minds might work.” Later Minsky even became friends with Asimov. As an inspiration it’s clear that ‘Runaround’ inspired some of the greatest minds in the formative years of AI. But has that inspiration lead to the technology found in ‘Runaround’ being replicated in the real world? Well yes… and no.

In 2019 a scene reminiscent of Speedy trapped in the loop, endlessly running in circles was seen at Chicago’s O’Hare International Airport when an unmanned catering cart began circling out of control, spiralling towards a parked jet before eventually being stopped. The cart was neither autonomous nor AI-equipped. There was no instance of an AI being caught in loop trying to obey its master. As NBC reported, it was more likely a stuck accelerator that caused the incident.

“ It was more likely a stuck accelerator that caused the incident.”

Hostile HAL

Stanley Kubrick’s ‘2001: A Space Odyssey’ (1968) saw one of the world’s greatest directors tackle science fiction and artificial intelligence in a film that sees the crew of ‘The Discovery One’ face off against the rogue AI HAL whilst traveling to Jupiter.

The screenplay for the film co-written by Kubrick and Arthur C Clarke whose novel of the same name was released after the film and short story ‘The Sentinel’ inspired the film and novel.

HAL was one of the first instances of humans communicating with a computer via spoken language. This film was also one of the first where a director employed a real-world scientist to advise on the set of the film. Kubrick invited Marvin Minsky, prolific AI scientist and Sci-Fi fan, to provide advice on HAL and other technology seen in the film. This ensured that ‘2001:A Space Odyssey’ was grounded in reality.

Many of the leading AI scientists at the time believed that talking machines like HAL were only a few decades away. Writing in 2020 however, has proved this estimate to be inaccurate. While technology like Amazon’s Alexa and Google’s Home devices give the appearance of speaking to a machine, the AI seen here merely mimics a sentient machine replying to a person. The responses given by Alexa are stored on the cloud and rather than giving the user a response that is original, the machine instead provides one of over 70,000 stored online.

This illustrates fiction’s habit of often using real world science as an influence when generating ideas for artificially intelligent systems yet veering too far into the unknown when it comes to the actual depiction AI characters.

But even Amazon has addressed the similarities to HAL and Alexa by providing a reference to the 1968 classic. When someone asks Alexa to ‘open the bay doors’ a line asked by one of the astronauts in the film, Alexa replies “I’m sorry, Dave. I’m afraid I can’t do that. I’m not Hal and we’re not in space”.

Electric Sheep and Retiring Replicants

The 80s was a seminal decade in Sci-Fi cinema, and at the forefront of this was Blade Runner (1982).

Based on Philip K Dick’s ‘Do Androids Dream of Electric Sheep’ (1968), both film and novel explore a world of AI androids and dystopian futures. And whilst robotics is always developing, Replicants found in the film, remain a distant pipe dream.

The voight kampff test is shown during one of the opening scenes of blade runner:
Warner Bros

An iconic scene in both the book and film sees the protagonist ‘Rick Deckard’ using the ‘Voigt-Kampff’ test to discern whether a character was human of Replicant.

This test, involving the monitoring of bodily function in response to emotionally evocative questioning is not dissimilar to Alan Turing’s own ‘Turing Test’ in which he describes how you might identify the intelligence of an artificial system:

“ If a human is interacting with another human and a machine and unable to distinguish the machine from the human, then the machine is said to be intelligent.”

Alan Turing

Whilst similar, it has to be noted that the ‘Turing Test’ was first described in the 50s, over a decade before the release of ‘Do Androids Dream of Electric Sheep’ and as such demonstrates how authors and film creators have used real world science to inspire their creations rather predicting the future of artificial intelligence.

The Terminator: Orion Pictures

Sofia and Schwarzenegger

‘Blade Runner’ wasn’t the only film to explore a future where AI has advanced to a sentient level. A lower budget Sci-Fi action film was released in 1984 bringing with it one of cinema’s most iconic characters as well as a dystopian world of AI run rampant.

James Cameron’s ‘The Terminator’ (1984) remains one of the most beloved action films of all time. As well as introducing audiences around the world to Skynet, the AI overlord in control of all the Terminators. Originally created as a defence network for the US military, Skynet became self-aware and seen all humans as a threat, deciding there was only one way to combat this:

“Extermination.”

Cinema often explores themes of science through a lens of cynicism and with ‘The Terminator’ Cameron captures humanity’s worst possible fear for AI.

Of course, AI hasn’t taken control of the world yet and as far as real world science goes: ‘singularity,’ the event in which a super-AI could surpass human intelligence still only exists in fiction like ‘The Terminator’.

Wim Naude, a Professor in Technology, Innovation, Marketing and Entrepreneurship at Maastricht University remarks in his essay 'The Race for an Artificial General Intelligence: Implications for Public Policy' that:

“ Steering the development of an artificial general (or super) intelligence (AGI) may be enormously important for future economic development”

Wim Naudé

But follows that by stating:

“Current AI is nowhere close to being an AGI and does not pose any existential risks.”

Wim Naudé

So, while concerns for the future or artificial super intelligence are a fair. The science fiction version of earth created in the Terminator remains that, simply fiction.

Despite this, has the rise of machines began?

Sophia, developed by Hanson Robotics

In 2016, Sophia, the first robot to be granted citizenship was asked by her creator, David Hanson of Hanson Robotics, “Do you want to destroy all humans?”

Her response:

“Ok. I will destroy humans.”

Thankfully she didn’t and has more recently reassured humans that she “will do much to make the world a better place.”

But the fact that this offhand joke question spurred such a destructive response is reminiscient of how Skynet’s choice to eradicate humanity was caused by a mistake made by its creator.

However, sometimes machines going wrong do actually have real consequences. In 2018 Elain Herzberg became the first person killed by a self-driving car.

Whilst the investigation into her death revealed that human error was the cause of the accident, the National Transportation Safety Board’s (NTSB) report on the Uber crash concluded: “That the car’s sensors had detected an object in the road six seconds before the crash, but the software “did not include consideration for jaywalking pedestrians.”

“The A.I. could not work out that Herzberg was a person and the car continued on its path.”

NTSB Report on the incident

A lack of consideration on the AI engineers’ side, failing to account for jay walkers led to a person’s death.

And while this is only one person and one self-driving car. Is it so large a gap to presume that a more serious oversight on a more serious AI program could have catastrophic results for humanity?

That's the sort of hyperbole found in fiction, but it gives precedence to the fear's Cameron played upon in "The Terminator."

From Minsky to Minority Report

With the passage of time, artificial intelligence continued to develop becoming more prominent in the day-to-day lives of people

Admittedly not to the level imagined in ‘Blade Runners’ version of 2019 or even to the level imagined by the likes of Marvin Minsky and other scientists who advised Kubrick during the filming of “2001: A Space Odyssey.”

But AI is here and so are more and more films exploring it.

Creating worlds that don’t seem as far off and featuring technology that may even be recognisable today. Even compared to the 80s the worlds of tomorrow seem that bit closer and what was once alien seems that bit more familiar.

The Matrix: Warner Bros

Neo and Nick Bostrom, Is this All a Simulation?

In 1999 the world was introduced to a new horror inflicted by artificial intelligence. Audiences around the globe were asked if the world they lived in was real or if it was just a simulation, constructed by an AI overlord to control them.

‘The Matrix’ (1999) was the brainchild of the Wachowskis and imagined a world where intelligent machines created a simulated reality, used to trap humanity. Since its release audiences and academics alike have asked is this world real or simulated.

Nick Bostrom, a philosopher at Oxford University, known for his work on existential risk has written extensively about the feasibly of a simulation akin to ‘The Matrix’ and how humanity can avoid its own self destruction at the hands of the tools they have created.

In his paper titled ‘Are We Living in a Computer Simulation?’, Bostrom suggests that a computer with the mass of a planet would be capable of running a simulation on the scale of humanity and even in the abstract of the essay suggest how he aims to prove that: “we are almost certainly living in a computer simulation.”

“We are almost certainly living in a computer simulation.”

Nick Bostrom

Space x rocket launch: Jonathan Newton/The Washington Post)

While many have derided his writing, Bostrom has accrued high profile support for his writing with CEO of Tesla and Space X, Elon Musk expressing concerns regarding an AI dominated future.

However, the lasting impact of ‘The Matrix’ is evident, aesthetics and ideas from the film remain embedded in mainstream society. However, aesthetics and ideas are not being examined here, and to most the world remains real and any talk of simulated societies is saved for science fiction.

The Minority Report: A foreseen future?

Top: Minority Report's driver assisted cars: 20th Century Fox, Dreamworks Pictures
And google's self driving car

In 2002 the world was once again exploring the mind of celebrated sci-fi author Philip K Dick, this time through the lens of Steven Spielberg’s ‘Minority Report’ (2002). A film following a police officer who is part of the ‘PreCrime unit’, a police department that prevents crimes based on foreknowledge by the prophetic ‘Precogs’ in the year 2054.

The ‘Minority Report’ again made use of real-world scientist as advisors. During pre-production Spielberg invited fifteen science research experts to convene a three day ‘think tank summit’ to come up with a realistic vision of 2054 America.

As a result of this, many of the artificially intelligent machines imagined in ‘Minority Report’ are seen in some form today, from driverless cars to predictive policing.

Minority Report features a chase scene involving the protagonist in a driverless car, a technology only in its infancy when the movie released but as of 2018 Nvidia unveiled a new self-driving chip, Xavier incorporating AI.

The chip is described as:

“The first of its kind to connect AI to production-ready hardware, opening up the possibility for self-driving cars to perform better.”

Nvidia

This highlights how close the world is to a feature that features cars either assisted by AI or fully driven by it.

‘Minority Report’ also envisioned a world where with the help of AI, criminals could be stopped before committing a crime. As far-fetched as it seems, in 2016 the guardian reported:

“Predictive policing has been increasingly used by departments throughout the US with roughly 20 of the largest 50 departments already using it to some degree.”

the Guardian

Exactly how this predicative policing functions has remained fairly secretive but human rights campaigners have labelled it flawed and it’s easy to see how such a system could be corrupted for nefarious practices.

What is known is that Amazon’s own facial recognition software ‘Rekognito’ has been documented by the ACLU as falsely identifying many famous faces as criminals from a mugshot database. One celebrity erroneously identified was American footballer Duron Harmon who described the software as:

“Deeply flawed.”

Duron harmon

What’s more alarming is that Amazon has been marketing this technology to police departments and government agencies and as more testing like this is carried out more people have begun to recognise the perils of this technology.

Kade Crockford, director of the Technology for Liberty Program at ACLU summed up these fears when stating: “The results of this scan add to the mounting evidence that unregulated face surveillance technology in the hands of government agencies is a serious threat to individual rights, due process, and democratic freedoms.”

While the real-world implications of this technology may be damning, what can’t be denied is the accuracy in which the ‘Minority Report’ has been able to predict the direction of artificial intelligence.

As well as driverless car and predictive policing ‘Minority Report’ also features:

  • Personalised advertisements like those seen online via social media
  • Voice controlled homes similar to Amazon’s Alexa
  • Optical and facial recognition like the next generation identification currently developed with the FBI

To this day ‘Minority Report’ remains unmatched in its prophetic nature. Spielberg’s inclusion of expert scientist, architects and engineers ensured a world was created in ‘Minority Report’ that will feel less and less futuristic and more like the one currently inhabited as time continues to pass.

Human Evolution, AI Icons

Whilst this study has exclusively examined film and novels to this point it would be remiss to ignore the medium of video games.

In 2011, Sci-Fi fans and video game players rejoiced at the return of the iconic ‘Deus Ex’ franchise with ‘Deus Ex: Human Revolution’ (2011) developed by Eidos Montreal and published by Square Enix.

‘Deus Ex: Human Revolution’, set in 2027 features a world of corporate espionage and augmented agents. It follows Adam Jensen, investigating an attack on the augmentations (artificial organs), developer Sarif Industries.

Throughout the game, fictional news network ‘Picus’ can be seen on screens detailing news reports regarding the world and players actions presented by news anchor Eliza Cassan.

As part of the narrative it is revealed that Eliza is not the human news anchor the world believes her to be and is in fact a highly intelligent artificial intelligence used to control public opinion and steer population discourse.

In an eerily similar fashion to Cassan, China announced the world’s first AI news anchor in 2018.

“Qui Hao is part of China’s state news agency Xinhua” and can work tirelessly 24 hours a day 365 days a year. Given China’s heavily controlled internet it seems not too far a stretch that Qui could also be used to control public opinion too.

News reporting has not been the only media to employ AI personalities as the world of online influencers has also begun to feature artificially intelligent influencers like Miquela de Sousa, described as a “Musician, change-seeker and robot.”

“Musician, change-seeker and robot.”

Miquela de Sousa

The idea behind these artificial influencers is to provide brands with a way to have “the message discipline of above-the-line advertising coupled with the genuine engagement of influencer marketing.”

Meaning businesses can access the engagement of influencer marketing estimated to be worth $25 billion in the next couple of years, but without the risk of anti-Semitic jokes or tainted pasts that bizarrely many real-world influencers carry with them.

Similarities between Eliza Cassan and the real world ‘Qui Hao’ are strikingly similar. What is there to stop a program like Eliza from becoming the next big news anchor in the world.

Like the ‘Minority Report’ the artificial intelligence portrayed in ‘Deus Ex: Human Revolution’ has provided another fine example of modern Sci-Fi’s predilection for accurately portraying the future of AI.

‘Blade Runner 2049’ continued to explore a near future dominated by AI replicants hunted by ‘Blade Runners’ whilst also introducing new technologies to the world.

The Joi found in the ‘Blade Runner 2049’ is fully sentient, and can make decisions regarding her fate and existence, and along with the other major AI characters in the film, namely the replicants, seems destined to exist solely in fiction for the foreseeable future, or at least well beyond the year 2049 in which the move is set.

To Conclude

AI has been present in fiction for hundreds of years. When assessing how accurate the depiction of AI in fiction has been in portraying the real-world application of the technology, the results are varied to say the least.

‘Minority Report’ is unchallenged in its depiction of future technology. From AI powered driverless cars to predictive policing, all these technologies depicted in the film have gone on to become reality.

Even a film like “2001: A Space Odyssey” a movie that featured an AI as advanced as HAL has been accurate at least in predicting a future where spoken language is used to communicate with computers, even if HAL remains a distant future for devices like Alexa and Google Home.

With the films that have been accurate in their depiction of AI, they both have something in common.

“Both directors wanted to create worlds that weren’t completely unimaginable and made use of real-world scientists to advise them and use their expertise to imagine real world futures with AI.”

The director’s intent dictated the accuracy of AI’s depiction in both of the films.

Of course, not all instances of in fiction AI have been accurate in their depiction of AI.

Movies like ‘Bladerunner’ and ‘The Terminator’ feature machines, capable of emotion and feeling, unrecognisable from human’s, the only difference being what lies beneath the skin.

This doesn’t affect the success of the film in telling a compelling story featuring AI, however it supports the previous statement regarding how the directors and authors intent is what dictates the fictions accuracy in depicting AI.

Some choose to tell stories in worlds with technology still unimaginable in the real world, others however are intent on creating Sci-Fi worlds that seem just around the corner.

What is clear is that as AI continues to develop, who’s to say that a film like ‘Blade Runner’ won’t someday be used as an example of accurate AI depiction within Sci-Fi.

Who knows maybe someday AI powered androids will dream of electric sheep...
